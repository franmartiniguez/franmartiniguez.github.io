Project 1

For my project, I started off with a brute force approach to align the images together. In this approach, I started off by shifting the green and red version of the images in order to try to align them to the orientation of the blue version. In order to figure out how far exactly I needed to shift in order to align them, I searched over a vast amount of possible pixel shifts, going one by one, scoring how well each shift made the alignment, then continuing on until I got to the end. In order to score these alignments, I used normalized cross-correlation, which in essence takes a normalized dot product between the two images (the green or red and the blue) and sees how similar they are on a scale from -1 (worst) to 1 (best). This approach worked fine for images with low resolution, as it was feasible to search over all the reasonable shifts and score them one by one. However, for images that were of much higher resolution, this approach would not work very well, as the search space of reasonable alignments would go from maybe a few thousand to the millions. So for the second part, I started off doing a pyramid approach based on the brute force approach. What I did was scale the images down by 2 and 8 fold on their resolution. I then first ran the same brute force algorithm on the 1/8th sized image to get shifts that were more or less right for the original sized. I then sized up the shifts by 4 times, applied them on the 1/2 sized image, and then ran the brute force algorithm on the 1/2 sized image, with the change being that the search space of my brute force shifts was reduced by a significant amount, to compensate for the fact that the alignments were at most only a few pixels off as they had already worked on the smaller version so well. I then repeated the process again, of upscaling the shifts and running the brute force algorithm, on the full-sized image. The only real issue I ran into was that my images weren't aligning as well when the resolution got higher, particularly the red images weren't as well aligned as the green. In order to solve this, I changed how I did the brute force algorithm slighly: instead of aligning both the green and red images to the blue image, I first aligned the green image to the blue and then I aligned the red image to the green. This resulted in much better alignment across the board.
